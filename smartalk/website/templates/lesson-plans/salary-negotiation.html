In this section, you will find the fundamental strategies to prepare for a salary negotiation, feel valued in your role, and get what you deserve.

Research is Key

Understand the market value for your role in your industry and location. Websites like Glassdoor, Payscale, or industry reports can provide valuable insights. Be ready to explain why you believe you deserve a higher salary.

Timing is Everything

Ideally, negotiate after you've received a job offer but before you've accepted it. This is when the employer is most motivated to hire you. If you're already employed, choose an appropriate time, such as during a performance review or when you've achieved significant results.

Practice Your Pitch

Clearly communicate your value to the organization. Highlight your accomplishments, skills, and any additional responsibilities you've taken on. Practice your talking points with a friend or mentor to build confidence.

Be Confident and Positive

Approach the negotiation with confidence, but remain positive and respectful. Focus on the value you bring to the organization rather than making it a confrontational conversation.

Consider the Whole Package

Don't just focus on the base salary. Consider other benefits such as bonuses, stock options, health care, retirement plans, and vacation time. Employers sometimes have flexibility in non-salary components.

Understand Your Priorities

Identify your "non-negotiables" and the areas where you might be more flexible. This will help you navigate the negotiation process more effectively.

This section describes the possible responses from your manager and how to react to each of them. Click on the heading to discover tips on what to say.

Immediate agreement

▾

Express gratitude and discuss the next steps, such as confirming the details in writing or scheduling a follow-up meeting to finalize the adjustment.

Request for justification

▾

Provide specific examples of your contributions, achievements, and additional responsibilities. Reference market research or industry standards to strengthen your argument.

Budget constraints

▾

Express understanding and ask if there is room for negotiation in other areas of the compensation package, such as bonuses, stock options, or additional benefits. Alternatively, inquire about the possibility of a gradual salary increase or performance-based incentives.

Counteroffer below expectations

▾

Express appreciation for the offer and ask for further consideration based on your research and contributions. Politely ask if there is room for further negotiation.

Here's how to handle the most difficult situations and what to do to get out of them in the best possible way. Click to find out the details.

Outright refusal

▾

Express understanding and ask for the reasons behind the decision. Inquire if there are specific goals or milestones you can work towards for future reconsideration.

Performance concerns

▾

Address the concerns directly and ask for specific feedback. Demonstrate your commitment to improving and discuss how you plan to address any perceived shortcomings.

Pressure to accept the offer

▾

Politely express the need to carefully consider the offer. Ask for some time to reflect and reiterate your excitement for the role and the company.

Silence or deflection

▾

Politely bring the conversation back to the negotiation, restating your commitment to the company and the importance of fair compensation. Ask for clarification on the next steps.

{# Sezione 5: For Entrepreneurs #} {% call info_card('for-entrepreneurs', 'Exercises for Entrepreneurs') %}

For entrepreneurs, negotiation isn't about salary, but about securing funding, partnerships, and clients. These exercises will help you practice and refine your pitch to build a more valuable business.

Exercise 1: The Value Proposition Canvas

▾

The Goal: Clearly articulate the value you offer a client or an investor.

Instructions:

  • Customer Profile: Identify your target customer and their "Jobs to be Done" (the tasks they need to complete). What are their "Pains" (risks, bad outcomes) and their "Gains" (benefits, positive outcomes) when doing these jobs?
  • Value Map: List the "Products & Services" your business offers. How do they act as "Pain Relievers" for the customer's struggles and as "Gain Creators" for their desired outcomes?
  • Connect the Dots: The key is to match your Pain Relievers and Gain Creators directly to the customer's Pains and Gains. This forms the core of your negotiation pitch.

Exercise 2: The Investor Pitch Deck

▾

The Goal: Practice communicating the value of your business in a high-stakes negotiation with potential investors.

Instructions:

  • Problem: Define the problem that you are solving clearly and concisely.
  • Solution: Present your product/service and how it solves the problem.
  • Market: Demonstrate that a large, growing market exists.
  • Traction: Share key metrics that prove your business is gaining momentum (e.g., users, revenue, growth rate).
  • Team: Highlight why your team is uniquely qualified to execute the plan.
  • The Ask: State exactly what you are asking for (e.g., "$500,000 for 10% equity") and how you will use the funds.

Exercise 3: The Pricing Tier

▾

The Goal: Prepare to negotiate different service levels, not just a single price point.

Instructions:

  • Tier 1 (Base): Define your simplest offering. What is the minimum service/product a client can get from you? What's the price?
  • Tier 2 (Growth): What features or services can you add to the base tier to increase its value? What does this tier cost?
  • Tier 3 (Premium): What is your top-tier, most comprehensive offering? What exclusive benefits or services does it include? What is its price?
  • Negotiation Practice: When a client says "your price is too high" during a negotiation, you can quickly pivot to a lower-tier offering instead of simply lowering your price, maintaining your value.
