What are collocations?

A collocation is a natural combination of words that frequently appear together in English. These pairings "sound right" to native speakers because of their common use.

Examples: make a decision, heavy rain, strong coffee.

Using collocations makes your English sound more natural and fluent.

What are intensifiers?

An intensifier is a word or phrase that strengthens or softens the meaning of another word, usually an adjective or adverb.

Example: very happy (stronger than happy).

Intensifiers allow you to show degrees of intensity: strong, weak, extreme, or subtle.

Types of Intensifiers

  • Amplifiers (make the meaning stronger): very, extremely, highly, absolutely, completely.
  • Downtoners (make the meaning weaker): somewhat, rather, slightly, kind of, a little.

{% call info_card('usage', '3. Explanation of Usage', initial_state='show') %}

Strong collocations: Certain adjectives almost always go with certain intensifiers. For example:

  • We say "deeply concerned" (not "strongly concerned").
  • We say "absolutely delighted" (not "very delighted").

Why? English has fixed patterns that sound natural to native speakers. Choosing the right intensifier makes your English more accurate and polished.

Common mistake: Overusing very. Instead of saying very angry, say absolutely furious. Instead of very cold, say bitterly cold.

{% call info_card('upgrade', '6. Speaking Activity: Upgrade the Sentence', initial_state='show') %}

Take these "basic" sentences and make them stronger by using collocations and intensifiers.

  • I was very happy when I got the job.
    → I was absolutely delighted when I got the job.
  • It was very cold during the conference in Chicago.
    → It was bitterly cold during the conference in Chicago.
  • The instructions were very clear.
    → The instructions were perfectly clear.
