These questions are designed to help you reflect on your leadership style.

{# Usiamo la classe master .lesson-card #}

Your Leadership Style

▾ {# Usiamo la freccia standard #}
  • How would you describe your leadership style?
  • What leadership qualities do you think are most important in your role?
  • How do you adapt your leadership style for different team members or situations?
  • Can you share an example where you had to change your leadership approach to better suit your team?
  • How do you handle conflicts within your team as a leader?

Reflecting on Good Leadership

▾
  • Who is a leader you admire and why?
  • Can you describe a situation where a leader you admire had a significant impact on your career?
  • What leadership traits did they show that you try to emulate in your own leadership?
  • How did their leadership style influence the culture of the organization?
  • What have you learned from a great leader that you still apply today?

Leadership Challenges and Growth

▾
  • What is the most challenging aspect of being a leader for you?
  • How do you measure your effectiveness as a leader?
  • How do you foster leadership qualities in others within your team?
  • Can you discuss a time you struggled as a leader and what you learned from that experience?
  • What steps do you take to continuously improve your leadership skills?

Leadership in Practice

▾
  • How do you balance the need for results with maintaining team morale?
  • What strategies do you use to motivate and inspire your team?
  • How do you approach the decision-making process as a leader?
  • How do you handle failure within your team or department?
  • What role does communication play in your leadership style, and how do you ensure it is effective?

Learn about some of the most common leadership styles.

Common Styles

▾
  • Autocratic: The leader makes decisions without consulting team members.
  • Democratic: The leader includes team members in the decision-making process.
  • Laissez-Faire: The leader provides minimal guidance, allowing team members to make their own decisions.
  • Transformational: The leader inspires and motivates team members to achieve high performance.
  • Transactional: The leader focuses on daily operations and uses rewards and punishments to motivate.
  • Servant: The leader prioritizes the well-being and development of their team members.
  • Charismatic: The leader has a magnetic personality that inspires and influences others.
  • Coaching: The leader focuses on developing their team members' skills through mentoring.
  • Visionary: The leader has a clear and compelling vision for the future of the organization.
  • Situational: The leader adapts their style based on the current situation and their team's needs.

Key Styles Summary

▾
  • Autocratic Leadership: The leader makes decisions independently and expects compliance without input from others.
  • Democratic (Participative) Leadership: The leader involves team members in the decision-making process, fostering collaboration and valuing their input.
  • Transformational Leadership: The leader inspires and motivates employees to achieve exceptional outcomes through a shared vision.
  • Transactional Leadership: The leader emphasizes structure, clear tasks, and rewards/punishments to manage performance and achieve goals.
  • Laissez-Faire Leadership: The leader provides minimal guidance, allowing team members to make decisions and manage their own work.
  • Servant Leadership: The leader prioritizes the team's needs, focusing on empowering and supporting employees to reach their full potential.
  • Situational Leadership: The leader adapts their style based on the team members' maturity, competence, and the specific situation.

Essential idioms and phrasal verbs for discussing leadership.

Idioms

▾
  • Lead by example: To show the way by acting in a manner that others can follow.
  • Take the reins: To take control or responsibility.
  • Call the shots: To be the person who is in control or organizing a situation.
  • Set the tone: To establish a particular mood or character for something.
  • Put one's foot down: To assert authority or take a firm stand.
  • Rally the troops: To gather and motivate people toward a common goal.

Phrasal Verbs

▾
  • Step up: To take on a responsibility or leadership role.
  • Take charge: To assume control or responsibility for a situation.
  • Bring out: To encourage or develop the best qualities in someone.
  • Stand out: To be noticeable, especially as a leader or in a leadership role.
  • Pull together: To work as a group to achieve a common goal, often led by a leader.
  • Carry out: To perform or do a task or plan, often as directed by a leader.
  • Back up: To support or endorse a decision or action taken by a leader.

Questions for a deeper conversation about leadership.

Questions About Leadership

▾
  • Reflecting on your leadership style, which of the described styles do you feel aligns most with your approach? Why?
  • Can you share an example of a time you had to adapt your leadership style to a specific situation or team dynamic? How did you approach that adaptation?
  • In your experience, how do different leadership styles impact team morale and performance?
  • Have you observed particular leadership styles that are more effective in certain contexts or industries? Why do you think that is?
  • How do you think your leadership style influences the culture and atmosphere of your team or organization?
  • Can you identify strengths or weaknesses in your leadership style? How do you leverage your strengths and address your weaknesses?
  • Reflecting on your colleagues and peers, what leadership styles do you observe to be most prevalent in your organization? How do they complement or contrast with your own?
  • Have you ever navigated challenges or conflicts within a team due to different leadership styles between managers or leaders? How did you handle those challenges?
  • What strategies do you use to foster collaboration and synergy among team members who may have different leadership styles or preferences?
  • How do you ensure your leadership style remains adaptable and responsive to the evolving needs of your team and organization?

Non-Leadership Positions

▾

  • Personal Experience & Observation
    • What makes someone a good leader in your opinion?
    • Have you ever worked with a truly great leader? What did they do that stood out?
    • Have you ever found yourself in a situation where you had to take charge, even if it wasn't your role? How did that go?
    • What is the difference between managing and leading? Can someone be a leader without a title?
  • Everyday Leadership
    • How can employees show leadership in their day-to-day work, even if they aren't in charge?
    • What are some small actions that make a big difference on a team?
    • What would you do if you saw something going wrong at work, but it wasn't your responsibility?
    • When is it right to take initiative, and when should you wait for instructions?
  • Growth & Development
    • What leadership skills would you like to develop, even if you don't want to become a manager?
    • Do you think good leaders are born or made? Why?
    • Is there anything you learned from a bad leader?
    • What is one leadership quality you admire and would like to practice more of?
  • Influence & Team Dynamics
    • How can someone positively influence others without being the boss?
    • How can leadership be shared within a team?
    • What role does trust play in leadership—even among team members?

Think about how you would handle these common leadership challenges.

Practice Scenarios

▾
  • Two team members are in a disagreement over project task allocation. The tension is impacting morale and productivity. How do you address the conflict?
  • A team member consistently misses deadlines and produces subpar work. How would you help them improve while maintaining a positive relationship?
  • Your team is made up of individuals from diverse backgrounds, and there is a lack of cohesion. How would you organize team-building activities to foster unity?
  • Your organization is implementing a new technology system. How would you communicate the change to your team and support them through the transition?
  • Your team has a challenging project with tight deadlines. How would you engage the team in setting clear goals?
  • A team member has expressed a desire for career growth. How would you provide them with constructive feedback and coaching?
  • Your team is facing an unexpected crisis. How would you guide them through the crisis?
  • You have a large project with multiple tasks. How would you delegate responsibilities among team members?
  • A team member went above and beyond to deliver exceptional results. How would you express appreciation in a meaningful way?
