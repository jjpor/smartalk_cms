Topic 1: Vague Feedback

▾

When feedback is vague, it can feel frustrating. You know you need to improve, but you don't know exactly how. Without specific examples, it's hard to know what success looks like. Vague feedback happens more often as you move up in a company. Leaders are busy, and feedback becomes softer, talking about things like "vision" or "alignment." Sometimes bosses are too overwhelmed or not trained to give clear feedback.

  • Have you ever been given vague feedback, like “be more strategic” or “do better”? How did you try to figure out what to do?
  • Why do you think managers sometimes give unclear advice?
{# Tendina interna 2 (chiusa) #}

Topic 2: Asking Better Questions

▾

To get better feedback, ask more specific questions. Instead of asking “How did I do?” you can ask “What's one thing I could have improved in my presentation?” Narrowing the focus helps your manager give you useful answers. Presenting two clear options (forced choice) can help managers give faster and clearer feedback. Instead of asking open questions, offer choices like “Is the issue the message or the audience?”

  • Why do specific questions lead to better feedback? Can you think of examples of “bad” and “good” feedback questions?
  • Why does giving two options make decision-making easier?
{# Tendina interna 3 (chiusa) #}

Topic 3: Non-Verbal Clues

▾

Sometimes your boss's body language (non-verbal signals) can show you their real feelings, even when their words are polite. Pay attention to facial expressions, tone, and where they focus their attention. It's OK to politely explain that you need more detailed feedback. You can say you learn better with examples or explain how a lack of clear feedback slows down your work. Leaders often don't realize the problem unless you tell them.

  • Why is it important to notice non-verbal signals? What body language have you noticed from bosses or coworkers before?
  • How can you politely ask for better feedback? Why might leaders not realize they are being unclear?

This exercise can be done with a partner or in a small group. Follow the steps below to practice giving and receiving feedback.

{# Tendina interna 1 (chiusa) #}

The Scenario

▾

Imagine you have just finished a project with a colleague. You are now having a meeting to discuss how the project went and to give each other feedback.

{# Tendina interna 2 (chiusa) #}

Exercise Steps

▾
{# Uso la classe .exercise-step dal CSS master, come nella "legenda" #}

Step 1: Set the Stage

One person is the "Manager," and the other is the "Employee." The Manager has to give vague feedback (e.g., "be more confident," "improve your communication"). The Employee's goal is to ask clarifying questions to get actionable advice.

Step 2: The Conversation

Role-play the conversation for 3-5 minutes. The Employee should use the techniques discussed earlier, such as asking specific questions or presenting forced choices.

Step 3: Debrief & Discuss

Switch roles and repeat the exercise. Afterward, discuss these questions together:

  • What was most challenging about giving or receiving vague feedback?
  • Did asking specific questions make it easier to get clear answers?
  • What did you learn from this role-play that you can apply at work?
